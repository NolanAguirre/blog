Alignment as a Practice

Management May 28, 2026

Alignment is a practice, not a state. People can be aligned, but that alignment is temporary. In order to maintain alignment over time, there must be some process in place, as minimal as required, to keep that alignment from decaying.

Communication is easy on paper

In theory, communication is easy. In practice, it is messy. Individual learnings rarely arrive cleanly packaged and ready to be shared. They happen in fragments: a customer says something weird, an implementation detail changes the shape of the problem, a constraint appears that was not obvious at the start, or someone realizes that the original plan does not quite fit reality.

The difficult part is not that people refuse to communicate. New context feels obvious once you have it. The hard part is recognizing that the rest of the team may still be operating from the older version of the problem.

Sometimes learnings are small, and it is not immediately clear how they affect the end goal. Deciding what is worth sharing is a judgment call. To use an intentionally extreme case, a developer does not need to communicate every line of code they write to the full business. But if they discover an edge case in an external API that changes the approach being taken, that is no longer just implementation detail. That is new context the team may need to understand.

Alignment decays

A fundamental part of iterative development is acknowledging that today is the day we know the least about the goals we are trying to accomplish. We may have a direction, a plan, and a shared understanding, but that understanding is still built on assumptions.

The same principle applies to alignment. Even when the team is aligned today, that alignment is based on the least complete version of the team’s understanding. As people learn more, the original alignment either has to be updated or it starts to decay.

Alignment naturally decays as learning happens and communication lags behind. Different people discover different parts of the problem at different times. Those discoveries improve the work, but they also create drift when they are not brought back into the team’s shared understanding.

Alignment is not agreement

People confuse alignment with everyone nodding in the same meeting. Agreement is easy to create in a room. Real alignment means people can leave that room, make independent decisions, and still move in the same direction.

If every decision has to be routed back through the same person, the team is not aligned. It is centralized. Alignment should reduce the need for constant permission, not increase it.

The purpose of process is to repair drift

The purpose of process is not to prevent change. It is to make sure that when the team learns something important, that learning makes its way back into the team’s shared understanding.

Good process creates a loop. Someone learns something, that learning is surfaced, the team updates its understanding, and the next round of decisions is made from better context. I believe this is the purpose of every meeting. The actual process will differ from context to context, but the goal should remain the same: keep the team’s understanding current enough that people can make good decisions without constantly asking for permission.

I have a heavy bias toward informal process: having a conversation instead of scheduling a meeting, writing a short note instead of creating a document, or quickly pulling the right people together instead of turning every update into ceremony. That requires a certain kind of culture, and it does not scale indefinitely. Eventually, a more formal approach has to be adopted. The important part is that the process should formalize the learning loop, not replace it.

Bad process tries to control people. Good process keeps people connected.

Too little process allows alignment to decay into confusion. Too much process turns alignment into ceremony. The right amount of process is the minimum amount required for the team to keep learning together.
